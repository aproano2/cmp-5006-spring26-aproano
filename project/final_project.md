# Final Project: Socio-Technical Impact Analysis
## Option A: This Is How They Tell Me the World Ends (Nicole Perlroth)
**Focus:** The ethics of the zero-day market and the militarization of code.
1. **The Ethics of the Shadow Market:** Analyze the transition of the "hacker" from a hobbyist/researcher to a state-funded asset. Students must trace the evolution of the zero-day market (from VUPEN to NSO Group) and argue whether the "stockpiling" of vulnerabilities by governments makes the global population more or less safe.
2. **Infrastructure as a Battlefield:** Using the book's accounts of attacks on power grids and water systems (e.g., Ukraine, Aurora), analyze the fragility of "legacy" industrial control systems. Students must evaluate the security debt inherent in modern infrastructure and propose a policy framework for "Active Defense."
3. **The Attribution Dilemma:** Focus on the difficulty of identifying state actors. Students should analyze the tradecraft mentioned in the book (false flags, obfuscation) and discuss how the inability to reliably attribute cyberattacks changes the way nations approach traditional international law and deterrence.
4. **The Supply Chain Butterfly Effect:** Analyze the "NotPetya" or "SolarWinds" incidents detailed in the text. Students must explain how a single localized exploit can cascade into a global catastrophe and evaluate the technical and legal responsibilities of software vendors to their downstream users.

## Option B: Tracers in the Dark (Andy Greenberg)
**Focus:** The myth of anonymity and the evolution of digital forensics.
1. **The Fallacy of Digital Anonymity:** Students must analyze the specific technical "leaks" that led to the downfall of AlphaBay or Hansa. Focus on how the permanent nature of the blockchain—initially seen as a shield—became the ultimate weapon for federal investigators (e.g., Chainalysis).
2. **The Global Jurisdictional Puzzle:** Focus on the international cooperation required to take down the Silk Road and subsequent markets. Students must analyze the conflict between local privacy laws (like the ones they are studying in Ecuador) and the necessity of cross-border data sharing in cyber-crime investigations.
3. **Cryptocurrency as a Social Catalyst:** Analyze the "double-edged sword" of crypto. On one side, financial freedom and privacy; on the other, the enablement of ransomware and human trafficking. Students must argue whether the societal cost of "dark markets" justifies the removal of financial privacy through "Know Your Customer" (KYC) regulations.
4. **Forensic Evolution & the "Cat-and-Mouse" Game:** Trace the technical progression of obfuscation (tumblers, mixers, Monero) versus the progression of law enforcement tools. Students must evaluate if "perfect" privacy is technically possible, or if human error (the "human element" in the book) will always be the primary vulnerability.

### The Deliverable: 

Students must submit a professional report (10–12 pages) containing:
1. Technical Breakdown: An explanation of at least two specific technologies/protocols mentioned in the book.
2. Societal Impact: How the events in the book changed law, public perception, or corporate behavior.
3. Personal Critique: How the book's narrative has shifted their personal ethics as a future InfoSec professional.
4. Peer Evaluation Rubric (Confidential): Each student must include a grade for each team member with their D2L submission. They will rate each other on a scale of 1–5 based on their participation. Consider the following points:
    - Did they read the assigned chapters and contribute unique insights?
    - Did they meet internal deadlines for drafting the report?
    - Was their technical analysis accurate and well-researched?
    - Did they help refine the ideas of others or just do their "part"?
